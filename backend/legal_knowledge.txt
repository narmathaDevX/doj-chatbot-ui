================ FIR & POLICE PROCEDURE =================

Section 154 CrPC defines FIR (First Information Report) as information relating to the commission of a cognizable offence recorded by a police officer.

A cognizable offence is one in which police may arrest without a warrant and begin investigation without prior court approval.

A non-cognizable offence requires court permission before investigation.

Zero FIR may be filed at any police station regardless of territorial jurisdiction and later transferred to the appropriate police station.

A copy of the FIR must be provided free of cost to the informant.

If police refuse to register an FIR, a complaint may be made to the Superintendent of Police under Section 154(3) CrPC.

If police still fail to act, a Magistrate may order investigation under Section 156(3) CrPC.

After registration of FIR, police investigate the matter under Section 156 CrPC.

After investigation, police file a charge sheet under Section 173 CrPC if evidence is sufficient.

Theft is a cognizable offence and requires registration of an FIR.


================ ARREST & POLICE POWERS =================

Police may arrest without warrant in cognizable offences.

Police cannot arrest arbitrarily and must follow legal procedure.

Article 21 guarantees the Right to Life and Personal Liberty.

Article 22 protects individuals against arbitrary arrest and detention.

Article 22(1) provides the right to be informed of the grounds of arrest.

Article 22(1) provides the right to consult and be defended by a lawyer.

Article 22(2) requires production before a magistrate within 24 hours of arrest, excluding travel time.

Section 57 CrPC prohibits detention beyond 24 hours without magistrate authorization.

Section 167 CrPC governs remand when investigation cannot be completed within 24 hours.

Detention beyond 24 hours without court approval may amount to illegal detention.

Police must prepare an arrest memo and inform a relative or friend of the arrested person.

An arrested person has the right to medical examination.

Women should not ordinarily be arrested after sunset except in exceptional circumstances.

Juveniles must be dealt with under the Juvenile Justice Act and not treated as adult offenders.


================ BAIL & RELEASE =================

Bailable offences allow bail as a matter of right.

In bailable offences, police or court must grant bail upon compliance with conditions.

Non-bailable offences require court discretion for grant of bail.

Section 437 CrPC governs bail in non-bailable offences by Magistrates.

Section 438 CrPC provides for anticipatory bail before arrest.

Section 439 CrPC gives special bail powers to High Courts and Sessions Courts.

Courts may impose conditions while granting bail.

Bail may be cancelled if conditions are violated.

A person may apply for regular bail after arrest.


================ WHAT TO DO IF RIGHTS ARE VIOLATED =================

If police refuse to register an FIR, approach the Superintendent of Police under Section 154(3) CrPC.

If illegal detention occurs, a writ of Habeas Corpus may be filed before High Court under Article 226 or Supreme Court under Article 32.

Violation of Fundamental Rights may be challenged in constitutional courts.

Legal aid services are available for persons who cannot afford a lawyer.

District Legal Services Authorities provide free legal aid.


================ COURT SYSTEM & REMEDIES =================

The hierarchy of courts in India includes:
- Supreme Court of India
- High Courts
- District Courts
- Magistrate Courts

Article 32 allows citizens to approach the Supreme Court for enforcement of Fundamental Rights.

Article 226 empowers High Courts to issue writs.

Judicial review allows courts to examine constitutionality of laws and government actions.

Appeals from lower courts may be filed in higher courts according to procedure.


================ CIVIL LAW BASICS =================

Civil cases involve disputes between individuals or organizations over legal rights and obligations.

Common civil remedies include damages, injunction, specific performance, rescission of contract, and declaratory relief.

Damages refer to monetary compensation awarded by a court.

An injunction is a court order directing a party to do or stop doing a specific act.

Specific performance requires a party to perform contractual obligations.

Rescission cancels a contract and restores parties to original position.

In criminal cases, the burden of proof is beyond reasonable doubt.

In civil cases, the burden of proof is based on preponderance of probabilities.


================ FUNDAMENTAL RIGHTS =================

Fundamental Rights include:

Article 14 – Right to Equality before law.

Article 19 – Right to Freedom including speech and expression.

Article 20 – Protection in respect of conviction for offences.

Article 21 – Protection of life and personal liberty.

Article 22 – Protection against arbitrary arrest and detention.

Article 32 – Right to Constitutional Remedies.

Violation of Fundamental Rights may be challenged before Supreme Court under Article 32 or High Courts under Article 226.

================ FAMILY LAW & DIVORCE =================

Divorce laws in India vary depending on religion and personal laws.

Under the Hindu Marriage Act, 1955, divorce may be granted on grounds such as cruelty, desertion, adultery, conversion, mental disorder, and other specified grounds.

Divorce by mutual consent is available under Section 13B of the Hindu Marriage Act.

In mutual consent divorce, both spouses must agree to dissolve the marriage and file a joint petition before the Family Court.

A cooling-off period of six months is generally required in mutual consent divorce, though courts may waive it in certain cases.

In contested divorce, one spouse files a petition and must prove legal grounds before the court.

Under Muslim law, divorce may occur through Talaq, Khula, or judicial divorce depending on circumstances.

Under the Special Marriage Act, 1954, interfaith marriages may seek divorce under specified grounds.

Maintenance may be claimed under Section 125 CrPC by a wife, child, or parents who are unable to maintain themselves.

Under the Hindu Marriage Act, courts may grant permanent alimony and maintenance.

Child custody is decided based on the welfare and best interest of the child.

Domestic violence victims may seek protection orders, residence orders, and maintenance under the Protection of Women from Domestic Violence Act, 2005.

Family Courts handle divorce, custody, and maintenance matters.

A decree of divorce is granted only after court proceedings and satisfaction of legal grounds.

================ DOMESTIC VIOLENCE & WOMEN’S RIGHTS =================

The Protection of Women from Domestic Violence Act, 2005 protects women from physical, emotional, verbal, economic, and sexual abuse.

A woman may seek protection orders, residence orders, maintenance, and compensation under this Act.

A complaint may be filed before a Magistrate or through a Protection Officer.

Domestic violence complaints may also be lodged with police.

Section 498A IPC deals with cruelty by husband or relatives of husband.

Cruelty includes harassment related to unlawful demands such as dowry.

Dowry harassment is a punishable offence under Indian law.

A woman has the right to reside in the shared household even if she does not own the property.

Stridhan refers to property or gifts given to a woman at the time of marriage and remains her absolute property.

Failure to return Stridhan may amount to criminal breach of trust.


================ MAINTENANCE & CHILD CUSTODY =================

Section 125 CrPC allows a wife, child, or parents who are unable to maintain themselves to claim maintenance.

Maintenance may be granted if the husband neglects or refuses to provide financial support.

Permanent alimony may be granted under the Hindu Marriage Act.

Child custody is determined based on the welfare and best interests of the child.

Courts may grant physical custody to one parent and visitation rights to the other.

Both parents may be required to contribute financially to the child’s upbringing.


================ PROPERTY & MATRIMONIAL RIGHTS =================

Marital property division depends on personal laws and court discretion.

India does not automatically divide property equally upon divorce.

Ownership generally depends on whose name the property is registered in.

Jointly owned property may be divided by agreement or court order.

A wife has a right to claim maintenance but not automatic ownership of husband's property unless legally transferred.


================ CYBERCRIME & ONLINE FRAUD =================

Cybercrime includes online fraud, identity theft, hacking, and cyber harassment.

Victims of cybercrime may file complaints at the nearest police station or through the National Cyber Crime Reporting Portal.

Online financial fraud should be reported immediately to the bank and cybercrime authorities.

IT Act, 2000 governs cyber offences in India.

Cyber stalking and online harassment are punishable offences.


================ CONSUMER RIGHTS =================

Consumers have rights under the Consumer Protection Act, 2019.

A consumer may file a complaint for defective goods, deficient services, unfair trade practices, or overcharging.

Consumer complaints may be filed before District, State, or National Consumer Commissions depending on claim amount.

Online consumer complaints may be filed through the e-Daakhil portal.

Compensation may include refund, replacement, or damages.


================ CHEQUE BOUNCE =================

Section 138 of the Negotiable Instruments Act deals with cheque bounce due to insufficient funds.

A legal notice must be sent within 30 days of receiving bank memo of dishonour.

The drawer must make payment within 15 days of receiving legal notice.

If payment is not made, a complaint may be filed before the Magistrate within the prescribed time.

Cheque bounce is a criminal offence and may result in fine or imprisonment.
